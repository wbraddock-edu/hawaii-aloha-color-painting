How to Prep Siding in a Tropical Climate Before Painting

By Aloha Color Painting Co. • April 25, 2025 • 6 min read

← Back to Blog

Why Prep Work Decides Everything

In the mainland painting industry, surface preparation typically accounts for 60-70% of a project's total labor. In Hawaii, we push that to 75-80%. The reason is simple: Hawaii's climate is unforgiving to paint that doesn't adhere perfectly, and the only way to ensure perfect adhesion is exceptional prep.

We've gone back and repainted homes where a previous contractor skipped steps. Every time, the cause of the failure was inadequate preparation. Here's how to do it right.

Step 1: Pressure Washing

All exterior painting projects at Aloha Color begin with thorough pressure washing of the entire surface. In Hawaii, exterior surfaces accumulate algae, mold, mildew, and salt deposits that are invisible to the naked eye but devastating to paint adhesion.

We use a 2,000-3,000 PSI pressure washer with the appropriate standoff distance for the siding material. Wood siding requires more caution than concrete block, but both need a complete clean. We always allow at least 24-48 hours drying time after washing before applying any primer or paint.

Step 2: Mildewcide Treatment

After washing, we apply a mildewcide solution to any areas showing signs of biological growth. Even if the visible growth is removed by pressure washing, mold spores remain in the substrate and can defeat a new paint job in months. A sodium hypochlorite-based mildewcide kills the remaining spores before we close the surface with primer.

Step 3: Scraping and Sanding

Any peeling, flaking, or chalking paint must be fully removed. We use hand scrapers, wire brushes, and palm sanders to feather old paint edges smooth. Leaving sharp paint edges creates visible lines in the new finish and weak points where moisture can infiltrate. In Hawaii's humidity, these weak points become failure points.

Step 4: Caulking and Repairs

All cracks, gaps at trim joints, and penetrations (utility lines, hose bibs) must be caulked with a high-quality, paintable exterior caulk rated for tropical climates. We look specifically for polyurethane or silicone-modified caulks with long-term flexibility — standard latex caulk dries brittle in Hawaii's heat and fails within a year.

Step 5: Priming

On bare wood, any spot-primed repairs, and stained surfaces, primer is essential. Spot-priming at minimum is required anywhere bare substrate is exposed. For complete repaints over poorly adhering old paint, full-coat priming is strongly recommended. Never skip primer on bare wood in Hawaii — the open wood grain will absorb moisture so aggressively that topcoats blister within months.

Done right, these five prep steps add 1-2 days to a typical project but extend the life of your paint job by years. Don't let any contractor skip them.
